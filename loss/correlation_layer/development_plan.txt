DSI LOSS SIGNAL CORRELATION LAYER – FULL SPECIFICATION
=======================================================

This document defines the complete technical specification for the DSI Loss Signal
Correlation Layer. It is designed for future implementation by an LLM or engineering
team. It is coverage-agnostic, actuarially valid, and fully aligned with DSI
principles: externally observable signals, machine-readable inputs, network authority
over self-reporting, and full auditability.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1 — PURPOSE
----------------------------------------------------------------------

The Loss Signal Correlation Layer provides a fully automated, DSI-native method for
inferring loss propensity without requiring historical loss data at the individual
risk level. It identifies signal patterns that correlate with loss frequency and
severity, enabling predictive loss assessment, cohort-based pricing adjustments,
and continuous risk monitoring.

The output is:
- Loss Propensity Score (0–100)
- Severity Propensity Score (0–100)
- Loss Propensity Band (very_low, low, moderate, elevated, high)
- Loss Confidence (0–1)
- Cohort Assignment (signal-derived peer group)
- Development Pattern (expected emergence timing)
- Loss-driven referral triggers
- Pricing adjustments (optional)

This layer does NOT replace risk scoring or exposure estimation. It runs in parallel
and provides an additional dimension for pricing and portfolio management through
explicit, auditable mechanisms.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2 — THE LOSS ANALYSIS PROBLEM
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Traditional loss analysis in insurance suffers from five fundamental constraints
that DSI is uniquely positioned to address:

2.1 DATA SPARSITY

Loss events are episodic. In most commercial lines:
- 80-90% of policies have zero claims in any given year
- Loss ratios are driven by a small number of large events
- Statistical significance requires volume that most portfolios lack
- Credibility-weighted approaches regress heavily to industry averages

Result: Individual risk loss prediction is actuarially impossible with traditional data.

2.2 FEATURE POVERTY

When losses occur, available features are limited to those captured at bind:
- Industry code (SIC/NAICS)
- Size metrics (revenue, employees, TIV)
- Geographic location
- Limit and deductible selection
- Years in business

These 5-10 features cannot explain loss variance. A bank with $10B in assets and
a bank with $10B in assets can have radically different loss profiles based on
factors invisible to traditional underwriting.

2.3 ATTRIBUTION IMPOSSIBILITY

With sparse outcomes and minimal features, causal attribution is nearly impossible:
- Was the loss driven by industry characteristics?
- Was it the company's size?
- Was it geographic concentration?
- Was it operational practices?
- Was it governance failures?

Traditional models cannot answer these questions. They can only establish weak
correlations at the portfolio level.

2.4 EMERGENCE LAG

Loss emergence timelines vary by coverage:
- Cyber: 6-12 months to full development
- D&O: 18-36 months for securities litigation
- Professional Indemnity: 24-48 months
- Marine: 12-24 months

By the time loss patterns are visible, the portfolio has turned over. The risks
that generated the losses are no longer in force. Learning is impossible.

2.5 AGGREGATION MASKING

Portfolio-level loss ratios hide segment performance:
- A 60% combined ratio may comprise 40% segments and 90% segments
- Profitable business subsidises unprofitable business
- Without feature-rich segmentation, cross-subsidisation is invisible
- Pricing remains market-driven rather than risk-driven

Result: Underwriters correctly state that "the gap between technical pricing and
loss is absolute."

----------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3 — THE DSI SOLUTION
----------------------------------------------------------------------

DSI fundamentally changes the equation through five mechanisms:

3.1 FEATURE RICHNESS

Instead of 5-10 features, DSI captures 200-400 signals per risk:
- Network authority patterns (50-80 signals)
- Technical infrastructure details (40-60 signals)
- Corporate digital footprint metrics (40-60 signals)
- Behavioural indicators (30-50 signals)
- Public record signals (20-40 signals)
- Structured data feeds (20-40 signals)

This feature richness enables statistical differentiation impossible with
traditional underwriting data.

3.2 BEHAVIOURAL CORRELATION

DSI signals describe HOW an organisation operates, not just WHAT it is:
- Security investment patterns (not just "has security")
- Management stability over time (not just "has management")
- Regulatory engagement history (not just "is regulated")
- Partner selectivity criteria (not just "has partners")

Behavioural patterns precede and predict loss events.

3.3 PRE-LOSS INDICATORS

Many DSI signals are leading indicators:
- Deteriorating security posture precedes breaches
- Management turnover precedes governance failures
- Regulatory warning letters precede enforcement actions
- Declining digital presence precedes operational failures

These signals enable detection BEFORE loss occurrence.

3.4 CONTINUOUS OBSERVATION

Unlike bind-time-only assessment, DSI enables:
- Monthly/quarterly signal refresh
- Trend detection (improving vs deteriorating)
- Anomaly identification (sudden changes)
- Mid-term portfolio recalibration

This transforms insurance from point-in-time to continuous risk assessment.

3.5 COHORT IDENTIFICATION

Signal patterns enable peer grouping independent of traditional classifications:
- "Companies with weak security but strong governance"
- "Companies with strong financials but poor operational discipline"
- "Companies with high network authority but technical debt"

These signal-derived cohorts may share loss characteristics invisible to
industry-code-based segmentation.

SECTION 4 — LOSS SIGNALS AND DATA CONTRACTS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Loss correlation signals use the same extractor infrastructure as risk signals.
Each signal must be externally observable, machine-readable, and reproducible.

All extractors return the standard structure:

signal_id: string
value: number | string | boolean
normalized_value: number (0–100) or null
confidence: number (0–1)
source_urls: list of strings
extracted_at: ISO8601 timestamp
notes: optional string

SECTION 7 — SCORING LOGIC
----------------------------------------------------------------------

7.1 SIGNAL NORMALISATION

Each loss-predictive signal is normalised to 0-100 using the configured normalizer.
For signals with negative correlation (higher value = lower risk), the score is
inverted: normalized = 100 - raw_normalized.

Normalization functions:

1. log_scale:
   score = min(100, log(value, base) * scale)

2. linear:
   score = 0 if value <= min
   score = 100 if value >= max
   else proportional

3. capped:
   score = min(value, cap) / cap * 100

4. percentile:
   score = percentile_rank(value, cohort_distribution)

5. categorical_map:
   score = mapping[value]

7.2 GROUP SCORING

For each correlation group:

frequency_group_score = weighted_average(
    feature.normalized_value * feature.weight
    for feature in group.features
    where feature.correlation_type in ['frequency', 'both']
)

severity_group_score = weighted_average(
    feature.normalized_value * feature.weight
    for feature in group.features
    where feature.correlation_type in ['severity', 'both']
)

group_confidence = weighted_average(feature.confidence for feature in group.features)

7.3 COMPOSITE LOSS PROPENSITY SCORE

loss_propensity_score = weighted_average(
    group.frequency_score * group.weight
    for group in correlation_groups
)

severity_propensity_score = weighted_average(
    group.severity_score * group.weight
    for group in correlation_groups
)

loss_confidence = weighted_average(
    group.confidence * group.weight
    for group in correlation_groups
)

7.4 BAND MAPPING

Two mapping methods:

1. fixed_threshold:
   Compare loss_propensity_score to min_score/max_score ranges defined in config.

2. cohort_quantile:
   Map score to percentile within cohort, then to band based on empirical
   loss distributions.

3. ml_derived:
   Use trained model to map signal pattern directly to band (requires
   calibration data).

SECTION 8 — COHORT ASSIGNMENT
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Cohorts are signal-derived peer groups that share loss characteristics.
Unlike industry codes, cohorts are defined by operational patterns.

8.1 CLUSTERING METHOD

Use k-means or hierarchical clustering on selected signal features:

cluster_features = [
    'security_header_completeness',
    'management_stability_index',
    'certification_authority_score',
    'credit_rating_trajectory',
    ...
]

Optimal cluster count determined by silhouette score or elbow method.

8.2 PREDEFINED COHORTS

For coverages with established patterns, predefined cohorts may be used:

predefined_cohorts:
  - id: "tech_mature"
    name: "Technology Mature"
    criteria:
      security_header_completeness: ">= 80"
      software_currency_index: ">= 70"
      management_stability_index: ">= 60"
      
  - id: "tech_immature"
    name: "Technology Immature"
    criteria:
      security_header_completeness: "< 50"
      software_currency_index: "< 40"

8.3 HYBRID METHOD

Combine predefined cohorts with clustering:
1. Assign to predefined cohort if criteria match
2. Cluster remaining entities
3. Monitor cohort stability over time

8.4 COHORT CALIBRATION

Each cohort stores empirical loss data once available:

cohort_calibration:
  cohort_id: "tech_mature"

SECTION 9 — PRICING INTEGRATION PATTERNS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Three supported patterns for integrating loss propensity into pricing:

PATTERN A — Multiplicative Adjustment (recommended)

base_premium = tier_based_premium(risk_tier, exposure_band)

loss_adjusted_premium = base_premium * combined_loss_modifier

Where:
combined_loss_modifier = (
    (frequency_multiplier * frequency_weight) +
    (severity_multiplier * severity_weight)
)

Caps and floors apply:
combined_loss_modifier = max(floor, min(cap, combined_loss_modifier))

Example:
- Tier 2 risk with elevated loss propensity
- Base premium: $50,000
- Frequency multiplier: 1.20
- Severity multiplier: 1.10
- Combined (50/50 weight): 1.15
- Loss-adjusted premium: $57,500


PATTERN B — Grid-Based Pricing

Pricing determined by (risk_tier, loss_propensity_band) matrix:

pricing_grid:
  tier_1:
    very_low: 0.0035
    low: 0.0040
    moderate: 0.0045
    elevated: 0.0055
    high: 0.0070
  tier_2:
    very_low: 0.0045
    low: 0.0050
    ...

This provides explicit rate differentiation by loss propensity.


PATTERN C — Three-Dimensional Grid

Full integration with exposure:

pricing_grid:
  tier_1:
    micro:
      very_low: 0.0030
      low: 0.0035
      ...
    small:
      very_low: 0.0035
      ...
    ...

SECTION 10 — CORRELATION MATRIX CONSTRUCTION
----------------------------------------------------------------------

The Loss Correlation Matrix captures empirically validated relationships between
signals and loss outcomes. Construction requires historical policy + loss data.

10.1 DATA REQUIREMENTS

For each historical policy:
- Policy ID
- Bind date
- Signal snapshot at bind (all available signals)
- Outcome:
  - Claim count
  - Incurred losses (by claim)
  - Development status
  - Cause codes
- Policy term and exposure

Minimum requirements:
- 1,000+ policies with full signal capture
- 24+ months of loss development
- Reasonable loss frequency (>5% for frequency analysis)

10.2 CORRELATION CALCULATION

For each signal:

frequency_correlation = pearson(
    signal_normalized_value,
    loss_indicator  # 0 or 1
)

severity_correlation = pearson(
    signal_normalized_value,
    loss_amount  # for policies with losses only
)

Additionally calculate:
- Spearman rank correlation (for non-linear relationships)
- Information value (for predictive power)
- Chi-square (for categorical signals)

10.3 STABILITY TESTING

Test correlation stability across:
- Time periods (rolling 12-month windows)
- Cohort segments
- Coverage types
- Geographic regions

Unstable correlations are flagged for review.

10.4 INTERACTION EFFECTS

Test for interaction effects between signals:

interaction_strength = correlation(
    signal_A * signal_B,
    loss_outcome
) - correlation(signal_A, loss_outcome) - correlation(signal_B, loss_outcome)

Significant interactions are captured in the model.

10.5 MATRIX OUTPUT

correlation_matrix:
  signals:
    - signal_id: "security_header_completeness"
      frequency_correlation: -0.23
      severity_correlation: -0.18
      information_value: 0.15
      stability_score: 0.89
      interaction_effects:
        - with_signal: "software_currency_index"
          interaction_strength: 0.12
      
    - signal_id: "management_stability_index"
      frequency_correlation: -0.31
      severity_correlation: -0.25
      information_value: 0.21
      stability_score: 0.92
      ...

SECTION 11 — CONTINUOUS MONITORING
----------------------------------------------------------------------

The Loss Signal Correlation Layer enables continuous risk monitoring,
transforming insurance from point-in-time to ongoing assessment.

11.1 REFRESH FREQUENCY

Configure signal refresh based on volatility:

monitoring:
  refresh_schedules:
    high_volatility:  # security, technical
      frequency: monthly
      signals: [security_header_completeness, tls_configuration_score, ...]
    medium_volatility:  # corporate footprint
      frequency: quarterly
      signals: [content_recency_score, hiring_activity_trend, ...]
    low_volatility:  # structured data
      frequency: semi_annually
      signals: [credit_rating_trajectory, esg_score_trend, ...]

11.2 DETERIORATION DETECTION

Alert when loss propensity increases significantly:

deterioration_rules:
  - condition: "loss_propensity_score_delta > 15"
    within_period: "90_days"
    action: "alert"
    severity: "warning"
    
  - condition: "loss_propensity_band changed from 'low' to 'elevated'"
    action: "alert"
    severity: "critical"
    
  - condition: "cohort_migration to higher_risk_cohort"
    action: "flag_for_review"

11.3 TREND ANALYSIS

Track signal trajectories over time:

trend_analysis:
  window: 180_days
  metrics:
    - score_velocity (points per month)
    - direction (improving, stable, deteriorating)
    - volatility (standard deviation of changes)
    - acceleration (change in velocity)

11.4 PROACTIVE INTERVENTION

Based on deterioration signals:

intervention_rules:
  - trigger: "deterioration_warning"
    action: "send_risk_improvement_resources"
    
  - trigger: "deterioration_critical"
    action: "underwriter_outreach"
    
  - trigger: "approaching_renewal AND deterioration_any"
    action: "flag_for_renewal_review"

SECTION 14 — VALIDATION AND BACKTESTING
----------------------------------------------------------------------

14.1 HOLDOUT VALIDATION

Reserve 20% of historical data for out-of-sample testing:
- Train correlation matrix on 80%
- Validate predictive power on 20%
- Measure lift, AUC, Gini coefficient

14.2 TEMPORAL VALIDATION

Test on sequential time periods:
- Train on years 1-2
- Validate on year 3
- Measure stability of correlations over time

14.3 COHORT VALIDATION

For each cohort:
- Compare predicted loss frequency to actual
- Compare predicted loss severity to actual
- Calculate chi-square goodness of fit
- Measure cohort stability (membership changes over time)

14.4 BACKTESTING METRICS

Metrics to track:

frequency_metrics:
  - lift_at_decile (top 10% should have highest frequency)
  - auc_roc (area under ROC curve)
  - gini_coefficient
  - ks_statistic (Kolmogorov-Smirnov)
  
severity_metrics:
  - correlation_with_actual
  - mean_absolute_error
  - median_absolute_error
  - percentile_accuracy

calibration_metrics:
  - predicted_vs_actual_ratio
  - confidence_interval_coverage
  - calibration_slope

14.5 RECALIBRATION TRIGGERS

Recalibrate correlation matrix when:
- Portfolio loss ratio deviates >10% from predicted
- Cohort performance deviates >15% from expected
- Signal availability changes significantly
- New loss data accumulates (quarterly refresh)
